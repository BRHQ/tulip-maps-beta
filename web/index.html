What it is

A studio for routes — and the roadbooks they become.

Draw a route on the map or bring in one you recorded, drag every point exactly where it belongs, add the notes and signs that matter, and turn the whole thing into a clear roadbook — the kind rally crews, tour organisers and road-trippers actually navigate by. Made by Virtual Marshal, and used on real events.

How it works

Three steps, no manual.

Draw or record

Trace the route on the map and it follows the roads — or import a drive you recorded on your phone. Town and street names fill themselves in as you go.

Tidy every turn

Drag any point to nudge it into place. Drop waypoints, notes and the official road signs exactly where they belong. Nothing locks — move it until it's right.

Share the roadbook

Each junction becomes a clear little diagram. Export a printable roadbook, a GPX that opens anywhere, or a .vmr — ready to follow, or to drop into a Virtual Marshal event.

What's inside

Everything a good roadbook needs.

A proper map studio under the hood — but calm enough to use at the kitchen table.

Draw on the map

Trace your route and it snaps to the roads, or lay it down point by point. The map does the hard part.

Edit every point

Drag any part of the line to move it. Nothing snaps shut on you — nudge it until the route is exactly right.

Waypoints & notes

Drop timing controls, notes and instructions along the way, number them, and pull in place names automatically.

Record on your phone

Drive, ride or walk the route and record the exact line — with photos and voice notes at the tricky bits.

Junction diagrams

Every turn becomes a clear little picture — a line for the road in, a bloom for the way out. Read at a glance.

Road signs & symbols

The official give-way, stop and warning signs, plus the markers a roadbook needs — dropped straight on a junction.

Check it against the track

Compares every diagram to the turn read from the GPS, and flags a wrong-way one before the whole field follows it.

A reusable library

Save the diagrams you draw and reuse them across events. Search by keyword — "hairpin", "fork", "roundabout" — and drop one in.

Export anywhere

A printable roadbook, a GPX that opens everywhere, or a .vmr that carries your waypoints straight into a Virtual Marshal event.

Why "Tulip"?

Because a good junction looks like a flower.

Those little turn pictures navigators read at a glance have a name — a stem for the road you're on, a bloom for the way out. Rally folk have always just called them tulips. That's the whole idea here: routes made as clear as a single flower.
